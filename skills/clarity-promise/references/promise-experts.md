# Promise stage — the sources

**Information foraging / information scent** — Peter Pirolli & Stuart Card,
"Information Foraging" (*Psychological Review*, 1999; developed at Xerox
PARC). Users forage for information the way animals forage for food:
they follow *scent* — proximal cues (link text, headlines, imagery) that
predict distal content. When scent weakens, they abandon the patch.
Application: the landing page is a new patch; the entry link's words are the
scent trail; the hero either strengthens it or breaks it. NN/g has
operationalized this for two decades: users decide in seconds whether a page
serves their goal, largely from the first screenful.

**Message match** — practitioner canon (conversion optimization, ad quality
scoring). Google's ad-quality system explicitly scores landing-page relevance
to the ad; the empirical practice literature (MarketingExperiments/MECLABS,
Unbounce) consistently finds headline-to-ad mismatch among the largest
single conversion leaks. Treat exact-words continuity as the default and
deviation as the thing needing justification.

**Five stages of awareness** — Eugene Schwartz, *Breakthrough Advertising*
(1966). The visitor is unaware / problem-aware / solution-aware /
product-aware / most-aware, and the *same copy cannot serve all five*. The
headline's job differs per stage: name the problem (problem-aware), name the
mechanism (solution-aware), name the differentiator and the deal
(product/most-aware). Auditing question: which stage does the primary entry
path deliver, and which stage does the hero address?

**Pre-suasion / priming** — Robert Cialdini, *Pre-Suasion* (2016). What is
made salient immediately before a message disproportionately frames its
evaluation (attention → presumed importance). The hero image and first
noun are not decoration; they select the frame. See also Kahneman, *Thinking,
Fast and Slow* (2011) on priming and WYSIATI — the visitor judges from what
is in front of them, not from what the site knows.

**First impressions form fast** — Gitte Lindgaard et al., "Attention web
designers: You have 50 milliseconds to make a good first impression!"
(*Behaviour & Information Technology*, 2006). Visual appeal judgments form
in ~50ms and anchor subsequent credibility judgments (halo). The promise
check is therefore partly visual: does the first paint *look like* the thing
promised (a developer tool should look like one).
