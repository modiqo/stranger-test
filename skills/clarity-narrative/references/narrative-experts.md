# Narrative stage — the sources

**The slippery slide** — Joseph Sugarman, *The Adweek Copywriting Handbook*
(2006, distilling his 1970s JS&A print work). Axiom: the sole purpose of the
first sentence is to get the second sentence read; every element (headline,
subhead, caption, first paragraph) exists only to keep the reader sliding.
"Greased chute" is the same image from direct-response tradition. The audit
translation: a section is judged not by its own quality but by whether it
receives the reader's momentum and passes it on.

**The curiosity gap** — George Loewenstein, "The Psychology of Curiosity"
(*Psychological Bulletin*, 1994): curiosity is the drive state produced by a
gap between what one knows and wants to know; opening a gap creates
attention, but an unpaid gap produces frustration and distrust. Honest use:
plant, then pay off on the same page.

**Open loops** — Bluma Zeigarnik (1927): interrupted tasks are remembered
better than completed ones. The narrative device of the planted-but-not-yet-
answered question borrows this; the audit counts unpaid loops as defects.

**Five stages of awareness (sequencing)** — Eugene Schwartz, *Breakthrough
Advertising* (1966): the argument must walk the reader *up* the awareness
ladder in order — problem, solution class, this product, this offer. A
section that answers a stage the reader hasn't reached is "premature"; one
that re-argues a completed stage is a "stall."

**Headlines carry the argument** — David Ogilvy, *Ogilvy on Advertising*
(1983): several times more people read the headline than the body copy. The
momentum test (headings alone must tell the story) operationalizes this;
NN/g's F-pattern scanning research (2006, re-confirmed 2017) supports it —
scanners consume headings and first lines disproportionately.

**Serial position** — Murdock (1962), primacy/recency: the first and last
sections are remembered; the middle sags. Put the strongest proof early, the
action last — and audit what currently occupies the two privileged slots.

**Momentum & processing fluency** — Kahneman, *Thinking, Fast and Slow*
(2011): cognitive ease correlates with believability; each section that
forces re-orientation ("wait, what is this about?") adds strain that is
charged against *trust*, not just attention.
