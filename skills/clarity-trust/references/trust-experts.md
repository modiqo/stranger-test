# Trust stage — the sources

**Web credibility** — B.J. Fogg et al., Stanford Web Credibility Project
(1999–2003); Fogg's **prominence-interpretation theory** (2003): credibility
judgments = what users notice (prominence) × how they evaluate it
(interpretation). In the lab, users assessed credibility largely from
*design look* and surface signals before content — which is why stale dates,
broken widgets, and hype styling are trust findings, not cosmetic ones.

**Specificity** — Claude Hopkins, *Scientific Advertising* (1923): "Platitudes
and generalities roll off the human understanding like water from a duck…
to say 'best in the world' makes no impression; to state a definite fact
carries weight." John Caples, *Tested Advertising Methods* (1932): measured
response consistently favors the specific claim. Modern support: precise
numbers are perceived as more credible and more likely to be estimate-based
than round ones (precision research in judgment literature, e.g., Mason
et al. 2013 on precise first offers anchoring harder).

**Influence** — Robert Cialdini, *Influence* (1984): authority (credible,
named expertise), social proof (what similar others verifiably do),
consistency, liking, scarcity, reciprocity. For audits, the operative
warning is that *fake* signals of these read as manipulation once detected
— detected fake social proof reverses sign.

**Perceived risk & guarantees** — Raymond Bauer (1960) introduced consumer
perceived risk; the direct-response tradition (Sugarman; Ogilvy) treats risk
reversal (guarantees, free trials) as the highest-leverage close because the
purchase decision is a risk decision. Placement matters: the reversal must
appear where the risk is *felt* — beside the price, beside the form.

**Loss framing of doubt** — Kahneman & Tversky, prospect theory (1979):
losses loom ~2× gains; the visitor's doubt is a loss forecast ("I'll pay and
regret"). Proof and reversal work by shrinking the forecast loss, which is
why they outperform additional gain-claims at the decision point.

**Trust arithmetic is asymmetric** — one inconsistency (numbers that
disagree, a claim contradicted elsewhere on the page) outweighs many proofs;
negativity bias (Baumeister et al., "Bad Is Stronger Than Good", 2001).
Hence the consistency check runs on the full number census, mechanically.
