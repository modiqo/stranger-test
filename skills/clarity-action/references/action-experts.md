# Action stage — the sources

**Fogg behavior model** — B.J. Fogg, "A Behavior Model for Persuasive
Design" (Persuasive Technology conference, 2009); *Tiny Habits* (2019).
B = MAP: behavior occurs when Motivation, Ability, and a Prompt converge
above the action threshold. Design implication ranked: increase ability
(make it easier) before motivating harder — motivation is volatile, ability
is designable. The CTA is the prompt; the audit asks whether ability is
maximized at the instant the prompt fires.

**Interaction cost** — Nielsen Norman Group: the sum of mental + physical
effort to reach a goal (clicks, waits, reads, form fields, context
switches). Krug, *Don't Make Me Think*: each click should be a "mindless
choice" — obvious, confidence-preserving. Labels that describe outcomes
("Start free trial — no card") preserve confidence; container words
("Get Started") spend it.

**Choice reaction time** — Hick's law (Hick 1952; Hyman 1953): decision
time rises with the log of alternatives. Applied: competing CTAs at the
decision moment tax exactly the resource the page just spent sections
building. Fitts's law (1954) for the physical layer: target size/distance —
primary actions deserve big, close targets (mobile especially).

**Checkout friction, quantified** — Baymard Institute (baymard.com), ongoing
large-N checkout research: average documented cart abandonment ≈70%; leading
self-reported causes: unexpected extra costs (~48%), forced account creation
(~24–26%), checkout too long/complex, upfront card requirements. General
lesson: surprise and premature commitment kill at the point of action;
most of it is design-fixable.

**Goal gradient** — Clark Hull (1932, rats); Kivetz, Urminsky & Zheng
(*JMR*, 2006, coffee cards): effort accelerates as the goal nears, and
*illusory* progress (a head start) accelerates completion. Visible steps
and started-states are honest applications.

**Defaults** — Johnson & Goldstein, "Do Defaults Save Lives?" (*Science*,
2003): defaults dominate outcomes at population scale. Ethical line for
audits: defaults that serve the user's stated goal are design; defaults
that serve the vendor against the user's likely intent (pre-checked
marketing, auto-renew burial) are dark patterns — flag as such (Brignull's
darkpatterns/deceptive.design taxonomy, 2010–).

**Commitment & consistency** — Cialdini (1984): small commitments grow;
foot-in-the-door works. Honest use: value-first micro-steps (run the demo,
see the result) before identity asks. Dishonest use: entrapment ladders
that escalate sunk cost before disclosing terms — a finding, always.
