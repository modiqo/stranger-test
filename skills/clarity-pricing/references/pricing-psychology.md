# Pricing stage — the sources and the failure catalog

## The behavioral foundations

**Anchoring** — Tversky & Kahneman, "Judgment under Uncertainty: Heuristics
and Biases" (*Science*, 1974); Kahneman, *Thinking, Fast and Slow* (2011).
Numeric judgments assimilate toward the first number considered, even when
it is arbitrary. On a pricing page the anchor is whatever price is seen
first — page order is therefore price perception. Precise anchors bind
harder than round ones (Janiszewski & Uy 2008; Mason et al. 2013).

**Prospect theory & loss aversion** — Kahneman & Tversky (*Econometrica*,
1979): value is reference-dependent; losses loom roughly twice gains.
Framing a price as avoiding loss ("stop re-buying the same exploration")
outpulls an equivalent gain frame — and penalty-framed pricing (overage
fees, downgrade punishments) is felt disproportionately.

**Mental accounting** — Richard Thaler, "Mental Accounting and Consumer
Choice" (*Marketing Science*, 1985): money is not fungible in the mind;
buyers budget in accounts. A price that lands in an existing account
("tooling per seat") is easier to accept than one creating a new account
("AI credits"). Also Thaler's acquisition vs transaction utility: the *deal's
fairness* is valued separately from the goods.

**Fairness / dual entitlement** — Kahneman, Knetsch & Thaler (*American
Economic Review*, 1986): buyers grant firms their reference profit but judge
price increases un-justified by cost as unfair — and punish them. Silent
redefinition of an existing plan's terms is the purest dual-entitlement
violation, which is why it detonates (see failure catalog).

**Pain of paying** — Prelec & Loewenstein, "The Red and the Black" (*Marketing
Science*, 1998): payments are felt as pain, modulated by salience and
coupling to consumption. Per-use meters couple pain to every action (taxi
meter effect) and suppress the usage the product needs to become a habit;
flat rates decouple it. A meter on the core loop is a behavioral tax.

**Decoy (asymmetric dominance)** — Dan Ariely, *Predictably Irrational*
(2008), the Economist print+web experiment; original effect: Huber, Payne &
Puto (1982). A dominated option shifts choice toward its dominating
neighbor. Documented as effective; audited here as manipulation — in
repeated-relationship (subscription) settings, detected manipulation
converts to churn.

**Compromise effect / tier architecture** — Itamar Simonson, "Choice Based on
Reasons" (*JCR*, 1989): the middle option gains share because it is easiest
to justify. Hermann Simon, *Confessions of the Pricing Man* (2015): price
*structure* (metric, fences, tiers) matters more than level; the value
metric should track the customer's realized value.

**Choice overload** — Barry Schwartz, *The Paradox of Choice* (2004);
Iyengar & Lepper's jam study (*JPSP*, 2000). Caveat honestly: the
Scheibehenne et al. (2010) meta-analysis found the effect conditional, not
universal — the robust prescription is not "always three options" but
"complexity of comparison, not count of options, is the load".

**Left-digit & charm pricing** — Thomas & Morwitz, "Penny Wise and Pound
Foolish" (*JCR*, 2005): $19 reads categorically under $20. Use consciously;
note the credibility tradeoff — round numbers signal quality/confidence in
premium contexts (Wadhwa & Zhang 2015).

## The SaaS failure catalog (audit against all eight)

1. **The invented unit** — credits/ACUs/tasks/points requiring conversion;
   the buyer cannot forecast a bill (violates mental accounting + unit
   legibility). Canonical: Devin's ACUs, Zapier tasks.
2. **Redefined "unlimited"** — the 2025 Cursor crisis: plan terms changed
   under subscribers, fine print in docs, $20 users seeing hundred-plus
   dollar months; public apology and refunds followed. Dual-entitlement
   violation; the category's trust reference point since.
3. **Metering the core loop** — charging per use of the action the product
   exists to encourage (pain-of-paying coupling; adoption tax).
4. **The decoy/center-stage theater** — a tier that exists to be worse, a
   pre-highlighted "most popular" rescuing users from engineered confusion.
5. **The checkmark matrix** — 40-row feature grids that convert comparison
   into surrender (complexity overload; Krug violation at the pricing page).
6. **Hidden worst case** — usage pricing shown at best-case rates with no
   exposure ceiling or example bills.
7. **Everything-is-Contact-Us** — no posted price for self-serve-sized
   buyers; forces a sales conversation the buyer reads as price
   discrimination (procurement friction, anchor vacuum).
8. **The quarterly fine-print drift** — allotments/definitions adjusted
   independent of the sticker price; each drift spends dual-entitlement
   trust that the sticker never earns back.

## The positive models

Flat, legible units of things buyers already count (seats: Linear); rates
expressed as multiples of a known plan (Claude Max's 5×/20×); free tiers
whose boundary is a *situation*, not a countdown; prices presented as
commitments ("nothing here gets walked back") — the anti-catalog position.
