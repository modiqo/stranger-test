# Fold stage — the sources

**Don't make me think** — Steve Krug, *Don't Make Me Think* (2000; 3rd ed.
2014). The founding text of the 5-second discipline: users don't read, they
scan; they satisfice (first reasonable option, not best option); every
question mark a page raises ("is this the button?", "what does that word
mean?") taxes a limited budget of goodwill. The fold audit is Krug's trilogy
— what is this / who is it for / what do I do — applied to the first
screenful only.

**Scrolling and attention** — Nielsen Norman Group, "Scrolling and
Attention" (original eyetracking 2010; updated 2018): ~57% of viewing time is
spent above the fold, ~74% in the first two screenfuls, with sharp decay
after. The fold is not dead (people do scroll) but the first screenful sets
whether they do. https://www.nngroup.com/articles/scrolling-and-attention/

**Illusion of completeness / false floor** — NN/g's term for a layout that
*looks* finished at the fold edge, suppressing scrolling; increasingly common
in minimalist designs whose generous whitespace composes as an ending. The
antidote is a signifier of continuation: content visibly cut off at the fold
line, a peeking next section, an explicit cue. A beautiful hero that fills
the viewport exactly is a conversion bug wearing a design award.

**Curse of knowledge** — Chip & Dan Heath, *Made to Stick* (2007), building
on Newton & Ross's 1990 "tappers and listeners" study: once you know, you
cannot simulate not-knowing. The builder is the worst judge of their own
hero copy. The SUCCESs framework (Simple, Unexpected, Concrete, Credible,
Emotional, Stories) from the same book is the stickiness rubric.

**Plain style** — Steven Pinker, *The Sense of Style* (2014): classic style
treats the reader as an equal who can see what you point at — nominalization
("zombie nouns") and hedging are how expertise curdles into opacity. William
Zinsser, *On Writing Well* (1976): clutter is the enemy; every word that
serves no function must go. John Caples, *Tested Advertising Methods* (1932)
for the empirical headline tradition: specific promise beats clever wordplay,
measured by response.

**First-paint halo** — Lindgaard et al. 2006 (50ms visual-appeal judgments)
and Fogg's prominence-interpretation theory: what is visually prominent gets
interpreted; the fold's most prominent element is therefore a *claim* about
what matters most, whether intended or not.
